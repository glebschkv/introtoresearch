Revision Exercise: Section A - ANSWERS
These are example questions that you could find in Section A – short answer questions on core knowledge / foundations of research methods and statistics. 
For each, I’ve provided an answer and a brief justification just to help with revision. You will not need to provide justification unless the question specifically asks.
1) A researcher gives participants the same questionnaire twice, two weeks apart, and compares the scores. What type of reliability is being assessed?
Answer: Test–retest reliability.
Justification: Measures stability over time by repeating the same test with the same participants.
2) Heart rate measured in beats per minute (BPM) is what level of measurement?
Answer: Ratio data (continuous data)
Justification: It is considered as continuous, ratio data because it has equal intervals and a true zero (0 BPM = no heartbeat).
3) A dataset shows that most values are bunched at the higher end of the scale, with a long tail stretching toward the smaller values. What does this suggest about the shape of the distribution?
Answer: It suggests the distribution is negatively skewed (left-skewed).
Justification: Most of the data falls at the higher end. But there is a long tail toward the lower values indicating a negative/left skew, because there are a few unusually low scores ‘dragging’ out the graph on the left. 
4) A participant’s z-score is −1.5. What does this tell you about their score relative to the mean?
Answer: It is 1.5 SD below the mean.
Justification: Negative z-scores indicate values below average (because of the negative sign).
5) A researcher reports a p-value of .82 for a statistical test. What does this p-value suggest about the evidence against the null hypothesis?
Answer: The result is non-significant. 
Justification: It suggests no / very weak evidence against the null hypothesis, meaning the data are highly consistent with H₀. This means there is no meaningful evidence to reject the null hypothesis.
6) In hypothesis testing, what is statistical power?
Answer: The probability of correctly detecting a real effect (1 − β).
Justification: Power reduces the likelihood of making a Type II error (a false negative).
7) A researcher concludes there is a difference between groups when none exists. What error is this?
Answer: Type I error.
Justification: incorrectly rejecting a true null hypothesis or concluding an effect or difference when in reality none exists (also known as a false positive).
8) You want to compare reaction times between two independent groups. Data are continuous, strongly non-normal and have extreme outliers. Which test is appropriate?
Answer: Mann-Whitney U test.
Justification: Nonparametric alternative to independent t-test – used for non-normal data and it is a more robust test for outliers.
9) If a researcher is running a paired-samples t-test, what assumptions have been met for the test to be appropriate?
Answer and Justification:
Independent observations: each pair of scores is independent from all other pairs, and each participant must have taken part in both conditions.
Normality – data from both conditions should be approximately normally distributed (or the difference between conditions should be approximately normal)
Type of data: the Dependent variable is continuous (interval, ratio or perhaps ordinal depending on the meaning/scale). 
Outliers: none or minimal (not extreme)
10) A researcher wants to look at the association between candle sales and spa music sales on Amazon. The data look monotonic, non-linear and one out of the two variables has a normal distribution. Which test is appropriate?  
Answer: Spearman’s rank correlation (ρ).
Justification: The data has a non-linear shape. Spearman’s correlation is appropriate when data show a monotonic trend and are not linear. And normality is violated in one of the variables so Pearson’s would not be appropriate here.

Exercise 1
Twelve participants were asked to solve a series of particularly difficult mathematical problems whose answers are all based on the same set of rules that are very difficult to explain. During the training session, participants were asked to solve 80 problems. In the test session, they had to solve a second set of 80 problems. For each session, the percentage of correct answers to the question asked was measured. The data are presented in the table below:

What is the IV? And the DV? 
(3 marks: 1 = correct IV, 1 = correct DV, 1 = correct measurement/units)
The IV is the Type of session (training and test)
The DV is the percentage of correct answers (ratio scale).
What is the design of the study?
(2 marks: correct design)
Each participants took part in all the sessions, therefore it is a within subjects/repeated measures. 
What inferential test the researcher should use? 
(2 marks: correct t-test)
The researcher is interested in the difference between the scores, the design is within subject, therefore, if the assumptions of parametric test are met, the researcher should perform a paired samples t-test
What is the alternative hypothesis?
(3 marks: 2 = correct alternative, 1 = correct direction of alternative)
For the alternative hypothesis, given that the researcher doesn’t specify a direction we can predict that there will be a difference in the percentage of correct responses between training and test.
The results of the test are presented below, summarize the findings and give a conclusion.
(8 marks: as follows:

All the assumptions of a parametric test were met: the DV is measured on a ratio scale, data are approximately normally distributed (W = .95, p = .705) and there were no outliers identified. A paired-samples t-test was used to determine whether there was a statistically significant mean difference between the percentage of correct answers between the training and the test phase. Results showed that that participants scored on average significantly higher in the test condition (M = 25.33, SD = 6.23) than the training condition (M = 20.42, SD = 6.29), (t(11) = 10.50,  p <.001, d = 3.03). These results suggest that performances improved from training compared to test phase.  
The researcher decided to run a two-tail test, explain why it is appropriate.
(2 marks: correct justification)
As there was no directional hypothesis given, a two-tailed test is the most appropriate.
Exercise 2
It is a very well-known phenomenon that if someone looks at an object, people will automatically look at the same object – this is referred to as ‘gaze cueing’. A group of researchers decided to look at this phenomenon using a classical Posner Cueing Task paradigm. They presented a central face to participants. The face looked to the same side (congruent), to the opposite side (incongruent) as a target or to a neutral (middle) position. They recorded whether participants saw the target (yes/no answer). The results are presented below. Assumptions of the test were met.
Table 2.
Frequency table of the participants’ answers and the type of cues
Results 
What type of data is this? 
(2 marks: correct data)
Data are Nominal
What kind of analysis would one use to analyse this data?
(3 marks: 1 = correct test, 1 = variables identified, 1 = categorical variables)
Here the researcher should use a Chi-square test of association (or 3x2 Chi Square test of association). The first variable is the type of cue, with three categories: congruent, incongruent or neutral. The second variable is whether the target is detected or not, with two categories.
The results of the test are presented below. How would the results be written up? 
(8 marks: as follows:
1 = correct variables (units)
1 = repeated the research question / aim
1 = assumptions met
1 = correct test reported 
1 = correct ‘line of results’
1 = state significance
1 = descriptives discussed
1 = interpret result)
Researchers measured how many participants noticed a target face (yes/no answer) that was next to a central face to participants where the face either looked to the same side (congruent), or to the opposite side (incongruent). All assumptions were met. The results of the Chi² Test of Association showed that there is a significant association between whether the target is detected or not (participant’s answer) and the type of cue (χ2 (2, 220) = 8.06, p = .018). For both participants who had / had not reported seeing the cue, there was a tendency to prefer the “Congruent” face, with 63 preferring the "Congruent" cue in the “Yes” response, and 51 preferring the “Congruent” cue in the "No" response, with observed counts deviating slightly from the expected values.
What would the researcher conclude from this output?
(3 marks: correct / additional descriptive interpretation)
For the neutral cue, there were more yes answers but less no answers than what was expected, whilst for the incongruent cues it is the reverse. There were less yes answers but more no answers than expected. For the congruent cues, the observed and expected counts is approximately the same. For both participants who had / had not reported seeing the cue, there was a tendency to prefer the “Congruent” faces.
Imagine that you were asked to redesign this study to have an experimental design. Which variable would you choose to change, and how would you change it? 
(4 marks: 1 = appropriate variable, 1 = number of conditions / levels discussed, 2 = how would you change the design)
You could redesign the study in a number of ways, but the easiest would probably be to make the eye gaze variable into the independent variable with three independent conditions: towards target, straight ahead, and away from target. The detection variable could then be changed into a continuous variable to become a dependent variable. For example, it could be time take to detect the stimuli (in milliseconds).

The supermarket owner wants to investigate the relationship between the number of checkouts and the average waiting time. Therefore, they performed a correlation.
Why is it the most appropriate?
(2 marks: correct justification)
The owner did not manipulate variables, they just measure two continuous variables at the same time. Looking at Figure 1 we can see that there is a linear relationship and no outliers. Thus, the supermarket owner should perform a Pearson correlation.

A supermarket owner wanted to investigate the average waiting time at checkouts to see if there was a relationship between the number of checkouts open and waiting time (in minutes). As seen in Figure 1, the relationship was linear, and both variables:  checkouts open (W = .95, p = .705) and Wait time (W = .50, p = .420) were normally distributed. A Pearson’s correlation test revealed a significant negative correlation between number of checkouts open and average waiting time (r(5) = -.92, p = .041).

Does the analysis support the supermarket owner’s prediction? 
(2 marks: 1 = yes answer, 1 = justification)
Yes, the prediction is supported because the owner also predicted a negative relationship and the data support this.

Revision Exercise: Section C - ANSWERS
In Section C you will be required to do a mini qualitative analysis – using thematic analysis. You won’t be required to do any ‘reflective’ thinking. You’ll be given a data extra (note, obviously not this one!) and will be asked the following questions.
Learning a New Hobby
“I started taking piano lessons this term because I wanted something creative to balance out my degree. I was nervous at first, but the tutor makes everything feel achievable. I like that progress is obvious - last month I couldn’t play a scale smoothly, and now I can get through a whole piece. Practising actually helps me switch off from studying... it’s the one time I’m not thinking about assessments. Sometimes I even stay after my lesson to try out little melodies. The data can be found on Ultra, ‘Dataset 1’
Type of Analysis: What type of analysis test was deemed most appropriate here? 
Wilcoxon signed-rank test
Assumptions Check: While Aiko checked the assumptions and found that the assumption of normality was violated, we should still check and decide if that’s the case. Report and interpret the assumptions of normality.
The Shapiro-Wilk test of normality was not significant for the hours spent of sleep per night for the group of participants (W = 0.96, p = .365). This suggests the data do not significantly differ to a normal distribution.
Based on the above assumption tests, create a Descriptives Table in JASP: Make it APA format and include the appropriate descriptives (for your analysis test). 
Table 1
Descriptive Statistics for the number of hours of sleep per night in a group of 30 adults. 
Based on your calculated assumptions above, run the appropriate analysis test and report the results (in APA).
Since the normality assumption was not significant, run the parametric equivalent of the Wilcoxon signed-rank test which is a One-Sample t-test instead (remember to set the known test value to 8 (i.e. 8 hours recommended sleep):
t (29) = 5.59, p < .001, d = 1.02
How would you interpret this result?
Our sample of adults had significantly more sleep (M = 10.03, SD = 1.98) compared to the recommended / average 8 hours of sleep per night, the effect size was also large.

ANSWERS
Carla compares the total number of new monthly subscribers of two streaming services, Netflix and Hulu. Assumptions of parametric tests were met.
The data can be found on Ultra, ‘Dataset 2’
Type of Analysis: What type of analysis test was deemed most appropriate here? 
Independent Samples t-test
Assumptions Check: While Carla stated the assumptions were met, we should still check and decide if that test is the correct one. Report and interpret the assumptions of normality and homogeneity of variance below.  
The Shapiro-Wilk test of normality was significant for both groups: Netflix (W = 0.65, p < .001) and Hulu (W = 0.63, p < .001) streaming services, suggesting the data differ from the normal distribution.
The Levene’s test of homogeneity of variance was not significant, F = 0.41, p = 0.527, suggesting there is equal variance between the two streaming service groups.
Based on the above assumption tests, create a Descriptives Table in JASP: Make it APA format and include the appropriate descriptives (for your analysis test). 
Table 1
Descriptive Statistics for the number of new monthly subscribers for the two streaming services (Netflix and Hulu)
Based on your calculated assumptions above, run the appropriate analysis test and report the results (in APA).
While the variance of the groups was similar (Levene’s test was not significant), the assumption of normality was violated (quite strongly) in both groups. Therefore, run the non-parametric equivalent of an Independent Samples t-test and do a Mann-Whitney U test instead:
U = 4.00, p < .001, rb = -0.94
How would you interpret this result?
There was a significant difference in the number of new monthly subscribers of two different streaming services (and the effect size was large), where Hulu had a greater number of subscribers compared to Netflix.

3. Research Design: Variables
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 21]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Experimental research
Defining Variables:
Independent Variable (IV): what we change or manipulate
Dependent Variable (DV): our outcome, what we measure
To see if changes to the IV affect the DV
Extraneous Variable: Other factors that could influence the DV 				 but are not the main focus (should be controlled for)
Confounding Variable: overlaps with the IV and affects the DV (potentially distorting the findings)
Demand characteristics: participants change their behaviour
Research Design: Variables
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 22]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
But experimental research is considered one of the more ‘controlled’ designs, right?
So how do we control these pesky extraneous variables?
To control for or eliminate the influence of these variables on the DV
Controlling for Extraneous variables
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 23]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Control groups: another group of participants that does not receive the manipulation to the IV
Randomization: randomly assign participants to different groups/conditions
Matching: pairing / grouping participants based on certain characteristics
Statistical control: statistical techniques to account for these effects
Holding variables constant: keep variables constant throughout the study
Pre-screen Participants: Ask participants to meet a certain criteria to help reduce variability
Controlling for Extraneous variables
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 24]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
A note on Operationalization
Operationalizing variables = making them measurable or quantifiable
Our variables need to be in a form that we can measure / test
Some constructs are not directly observable (e.g., anxiety)
e.g., we can’t really measure ‘hallucinations’, but we can measure certain factors (e.g., frequency, mode, content)

Reporting Assumptions
You don’t typically report/explain that each assumption was checked when assumptions were met. It’s assumed you’ve checked it and ran the appropriate test – so make sure you do!
However, unless.
we specifically ask you to report them (e.g. in exams)
you violated assumptions so are using a Non-Parametric test (generally good practice)
you found/removed/kept outliers (best practice to tell the reader what you did with them)
you transformed the data, or are using corrected values or (more in upcoming weeks)
a dissertation supervisor, reviewer, editor or colleagues request it
Check: papers of interest, read analyses/results sections – notice how many report assumptions if they are met. Notice when/how it’s written if assumptions are violated.

3. Content & Thematic Analysis
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 21]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Quantifies specific aspects / patterns (e.g., presence / frequency of words)
Codes – short phrases /words used by the participant
Analyses the context that these appear
Often interested in the content – what is explicitly stated
Results: quantifiable data (e.g., counts or %)
Draws inferences to understand broad societal / cultural trends
Applied to various formats (text, video etc.)
A more structured process (often based on predefined criteria)
(e.g. counting occurrences of specific words / phrases)
Typically deductive (predefined categories or theory-driven) (but can be inductive)
Allows for generalisable conclusions – leans towards replicability and objectivity
Content Analysis
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 22]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Identifies themes / patterns in the data – how people make sense of something
Codes – short phrases /words used by the participant
Depth over breadth (rich and descriptive understanding)
Descriptive insight and subjective experiences, perceptions and emotion
Looks at latent meaning – underlying ideas, assumptions
Flexible and exploratory (typically no predefined categories)
Purely qual – no statistical/numerical values
Narrative / descriptive output
Typically, inductive (data-driven)
Findings / patterns are ‘created’ by researcher
Subjective / interpretation
Reflection
Thematic Analysis
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 23]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Content vs Thematic (when to use what)
Feature | Content Analysis | Thematic Analysis
Purpose | Quantify and categorise content. | To explore and interpret patterns of meaning.
Codes | Typically, manifest (explicit) content (but can also be latent) | Both semantic (explicit meaning) and latent (underlying meaning)
Coding approach | Often deductive (predefined categories) but can be inductive | Often inductive (finding themes) but can be deductive
Focus & Output | Counts, categories, trends | Meaning-making – themes, narratives, interpretations
Flexibility | Structured and replicable | Flexible and iterative
Subjectivity | Minimised | Embraced – acknowledges and seen as part of analysis
Reflection | Some – procedural (coding consistency, bias reduction) | Central (own assumptions, iterative thinking/creative, interpretation)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 24]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Thematic Analysis: Braun and Clarke’s SIX STEP MODEL
Data Familiarisation
Initial Coding generation
Search for Themes
Review of Themes
Theme Definition and labelling
Report writing
- read and immerse yourself in the data
- for each participant, select key points/phrases or words into ‘codes’
- merge all codes into groups (themes) which have the same concept / idea
- review and refine themes (merge, create, change delete etc.)
- define the essence of each theme - what does it capture? Give themes a label.
- disseminate

[Slide 5]

[Slide 6]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Experimental Design
To determine the relationship between an independent variable (IV) and a dependent (DV) variable.
The IV(s) are manipulated, participants are assigned to different groups or conditions, and the DV is measured (also called the outcome variable).
Definition: Measures the size of the difference between these two groups or conditions (based on the IV manipulation)
Allows us to draw conclusions and cause-and-effect relationships (something that we couldn’t do with correlation)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 7]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What is a t-test?
Written as t
Definition: an inferential statistical test used to determine whether there is a significant difference between the means of two groups.
Purpose: assesses whether the average score of a dependent (outcome) reliably differs between two groups or conditions.
E.g., whether stress levels differ between participants who slept 8+ hours compared to those who slept 6 hours.
Test Output:
Calculates an observed test-statistic (t): the size of the difference between group means, relative to variability/noise. Positive or negative (sign indicates direction of the difference)
Corresponding p-value indicates whether the observed t-statistic is statistically significant (if p < α = significant, reject H₀)
Effect size (when selected): size (magnitude) of effect in the population – a more practical interpretation.
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 8]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
However, a t-test isn’t just about a cause-and-effect relationship…
It doesn’t just look at the difference between groups… but also their variability.
That is, it depends on how well-controlled our experiment / variables are. We must isolate and measure the effect of our manipulation (IV) on the DV as much as possible, to be confident that our findings are due to the IV and not some other factor
Remember the variability of data?  (Term 1, Week 5)
What is a t-test?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 9]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
A difference between two groups might only be meaningful if the scores in each group are consistent (low variability / low noise).
If the data from one or both groups are noisy and moving around a lot anyway (high variability) the average difference between them could just be due to randomness (not your manipulation).
We want our manipulation (IV) to have a consistent effect on all participants. For any difference between groups to be (mostly) explained by our experimental manipulation.
Variability of Data
Significant difference between two groups = larger ‘between’ group differences and smaller ‘within’ group variance.
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 10]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
At both Universities, there is change between our Treatment vs Placebo groups
= Higher scores in Treatment vs.  Placebo (yay!)
Variability of Data
Treatment vs Placebo in two groups – at two different Universities
So, both Universities had a significant cause-effect!.... Yes??
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 11]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The spread of data within each group, shows the Universities are different.
= University 2 variable / noisy across Placebo & Treatment groups (boo!)
Variability of Data
Actually, NO!
For University 2 we don’t know what’s driving the difference – there’s too much spread / noise, participants overlap. We can’t be sure the cause is treatment.
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 12]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
There will always be some difference between groups (some within group variance) we cannot control (e.g. natural variation) – even if you test the same people!
But we want our manipulation to have a consistent effect on all participants.
Are any observed changes / effects in the data explained (mostly) by our experimental manipulation (the between group variance)
That is - was the manipulation successful?
Variability of Data
We can do this by:
increasing our between-group variance and
decreasing our within-group variance
Remember we can’t test everyone – we infer or estimate confidence that our findings (manipulations) are a real effect (or rare under the null hypothesis)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 13]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
A t-test compares the scores of two groups/conditions, is it a big enough difference compared to the variability (spread) that is found within the group/condition naturally.
That is: is our experimental manipulation (of the IV) causing (significant) changes in the DV?
Once you take into account the variability or possible changes that may occur naturally within groups.
What a t-test tells you
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 14]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The greater the difference between groups/conditions compared to the variability = the larger the t-statistic (t) will be.
Large t = statistically significant result
We want a large/extreme t-statistic value (rare / unique) to reject the null hypothesis (which claims there is no significant difference between groups)
A significant t-test tells you
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 15]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Again, this is an important first step: visualise the data to explore the differences between groups/conditions
When dealing with group differences, a boxplot (box and whisker plot) or bar graph is best to:
see difference between the groups so you can compare the range of scores, level of noise etc.
general impressions of whether the groups are similarly distributed (e.g., normal or skewed)
Boxplot = shows the median, quartiles (ranges) and variability
Bar graph = show the mean, and with error bars (standard deviation) shows variability)
Identify outliers - which can change, cause or hide differences
The importance of visualising data
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 16]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Hypothesis testing
We use t-tests in our hypothesis testing… like Correlation and Chi-square
Alternative, One-tailed (directional) = where the difference lies
(e.g. Group A will have faster RTs than Group B)
Alternative, Two-tailed (non-directional, the default) = a difference exists
(e.g., there will be a difference between Group A and Group B RTs)
Null Hypothesis = no difference in RTs between Group A and Group B
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 17]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
So why do we use it?
T-tests might not tell us precisely why the groups differ (depends: well-controlled experiments can support causal claims) but they are still incredibly useful and widely used.
It tells us:
Are the two groups different?
In what direction?
How large is the difference?
Is the difference surprising under a ‘no difference’ model
Benefits of t-tests
Benefits
Identify differences between groups/ conditions
Test hypotheses about whether groups truly differ
Experimental – controlled conditions
Cause-and-effect interpretations (size of the effect)
Make predictions about expected group differences (e.g. treatment vs control)
Starting point for additional / other experiments, or other possible interactions

Feature | Parametric tests | Non-Parametric tests
Assumptions | Required (variance). Fairly robust to mild violations (e.g. if N > 30 per group) | Fewer or no assumptions required (independent observations is still required)
Data Type | Continuous data - Interval or ratio (or Ordinal data) | Ordinal data or data that violates assumptions
Use when | Data are (approximately) normally distributed | Data can be non-normal, skewed or contain outliers
Power | More powerful (when assumptions are met) | Less powerful (but more robust and flexible in non-normal conditions)
Effect size | Better at detecting smaller effect sizes if they exist | Rank-based or probabilistic: may require larger effects to reach significance
Descriptives | Compare group means | Compare group medians or distribution / position of ranks
Interpretation | Of mean differences | Differences in ranks of ordered data
Graph | Bar graph (common) or boxplot Mean and SD | Boxplot (common) or violin plot Median and IQR

Reporting t-tests
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 7]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
As you start reading more research papers – especially the results sections, you’ll likely note that different authors report statistics in various styles!
Where is alpha?
You might spend ages searching for the study’s alpha level (the significance threshold, usually p = .05) … but alas! Most authors don’t mention it at all.
That’s because:
Unless stated otherwise, everyone assumes alpha = .05.
If p < .05 → the result is considered statistically significant
Sometimes researchers use a stricter alpha level, such as .01 or .001, but this will be clearly stated (well, should be!)
General note on published results
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 8]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
You may also see something like:
all p’s < .042     or     p-values < .042
This is shorthand way of reporting multiple tests at once.
It means:
They ran several related statistical tests (e.g. a few t-tests) and all of the p-values were less than or equal to .042 – which means they’re all below .05, so they are all statistically significant.
It’s used to:
keep the text tidy and concise when discussing a group of similar tests
keep things concise if you have a lot of different analyses
when these tests are not the main focus of the study (e.g. supporting or background analyses).
While shorthand is common in published papers, you should stick to full reporting in your assignments. Because we like to see it written in full - to check your understanding, and APA format
General note on published results
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 9]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
You need to write your own summary of the results: including the ‘line’ of results for the analysis test (JASP output alone isn’t sufficient)

Reporting Assumptions
You don’t typically report/explain that every assumption was checked or discuss them in detail – we only report those that matter for interpretation.
It’s assumed (and important) you’ve checked all assumptions and ran the appropriate test – they do matter, so make sure you check!
You report assumptions if:
you are specifically asked to report them (e.g. in exams)
you violated assumptions e.g., you are using a Non-Parametric test
you found/removed/kept outliers (best practice to tell the reader what you did with them)
you transformed the data, or are using any corrections (e.g. Welch)
a dissertation supervisor, reviewer, editor or colleague requests it
Often, it’s a brief sentence and a result (e.g. of a Shapiro-Wilk test)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 11]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Reporting Assumptions
It matters, but we don’t report it. We assumed you’ve checked / done this when designing and collecting data (part of good practice)
Report the Shapiro-Wilk statistic and p-value for each group. One short sentence is enough or in a descriptives table. Don’t include the graphs (distributions or histograms) for each group.
If applicable (for the test), include a brief sentence to say it was met or violated - report Levene’s test (value and p-value). If violated, say you used Welch’s correction.

Writing the Discussion
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 39]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Limitations of the study. This should be shorter than the rest of the Discussion!
Think of smaller issues in design, collection, sample (characteristics) or data (e.g. merging, range)  that could’ve affected findings.
Don’t heavily criticise your study! Remember what I said about critiquing studies – same applies here.
Don’t critique without evidence or support (e.g. use studies / authors who have found or argued the same).
Good limitation: justify or offer alternatives – for example, if you mention your study had a low sample size, why might this be important or affect the findings? Would a larger sample size reveal a different effect, why?
Excellent limitation: justify and evidence claim. For example, introduce studies that didn’t have this limitation (e.g. a very similar study, with a larger sample size/better design who found a significant/expected result).
Keep evaluation balanced, not overly negative (you could include strengths?)

It’s brief (e.g., one paragraph) and could be integrated with limitations.

Introduction to chi-square test
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 7]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What is Chi-square?
Written as X2
Definition: an inferential statistical test to determine whether there is a significant association between nominal (categorical) variables.
Purpose: compares observed data (counts / frequencies) to expected frequencies under a specific hypothesis
E.g., or the frequency of occurrence (of a behaviour) between two categorical groups
Test Output:
- Calculates a test statistic χ2 which is then compared against a critical value to compare against a theoretical distribution –decision based on a critical value / p-value
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 8]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What is Chi-square?
Types:
(depends on aim and how many variables you have)
Chi-square goodness-of-fit: if data fits a theoretical distribution
when you have one variable
Chi-square test of association: if two nominal variables are related
when you have two variables
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 9]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
For both types of Chi-square test, it measures how far the data deviate from expectation (or from another variable)
Alternative hypothesis is always non-directional  (does not specify a direction)
It doesn’t tell us about direction, or make any expectations on which group/condition is bigger or higher etc.
However, the test uses a one-tailed critical region in terms of its distribution, because χ² values are always positive
Larger values indicate greater deviation from the null hypothesis
Chi-square test
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 10]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
When to use Chi-square?
Nominal (categorical) data
One or two variables
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 11]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Assumptions for Chi-square
1. Data Type: Nominal (categorical) variables
Type of data / level of measurement of all the variables should be nominal or ordinal (not continuous)
e.g., Hair colour, Political party
2. Mutual Exclusivity
Variables / categories should be mutually exclusive
Each participant, event, or item must contribute to only one category (one cell in the table). They must be independent.
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 12]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. Expected Frequencies
The Expected frequency for each cell should be 5 or more to ensure validity of the chi-square test
If any expected count is less than 5, a larger sample or Fisher's Exact Test may be more appropriate
A more relaxed guideline: at least 80% have a value of 5 >
Or no cell should have an expected frequency of 0
Assumptions for Chi-square
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 13]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. Independence of observations
Every observation in the dataset is independent. One observation does not affect the value of any other observation.
5. Random Sampling
Data from a random sample to ensure representativeness and avoid bias
Assumptions for Chi-square
Violating these assumptions can lead to inaccurate results, as the test relies on approximations that depend on these conditions
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 14]

1. O: Observed data

(total number of participants

5. (O – E)2 : Divide the squared residuals by Expected data
E
6. Repeat for all categories and Sum (∑) them
X2 = chi-square statistic

4. Interpretation and write-up
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 42]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Run the chi-square tests in JASP
Follow the “How to” JASP videos
(online content)
For either type of chi-square test, the JASP analysis output gives you a test statistic (X2) value and with it, it’s associated p-value for you to interpret
p-value = probability of obtaining this X2  value (test statistic) assuming the Null Hypothesis (H0) is true
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
[Slide 43]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Remember we’re testing a sample (from a population) to infer something about the population
Null hypothesis: no difference (between observed and expected frequencies)
Compare the calculated p-value to a set a threshold alpha level (𝛼), almost always 𝛼 = .05 (reduces the likelihood of making a Type I error)
And power (β) often = 80 (to reduce the likelihood of making a Type II error)
If p < α → reject the null hypothesis (there is some difference)
If p ≥ α → fail to reject the null hypothesis (insufficient evidence to conclude there is an effect)
